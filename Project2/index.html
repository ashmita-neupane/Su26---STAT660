Project Overview

This project analyzes customer behavior using an e-commerce dataset containing customer demographics, purchasing activity, membership information, satisfaction levels, and spending patterns. The goal of the analysis is to better understand the factors that influence customer spending and identify trends across different customer groups.

The dataset originally contained 350 customer records and 11 variables. During data cleaning, two missing values in the Satisfaction Level column were identified and removed, resulting in 348 observations used for analysis. Several variables were renamed, and age groups were created to examine spending patterns across different age ranges.

The visualizations explore relationships between customer characteristics and purchasing behavior, including membership type, age, city, gender, discounts, satisfaction levels, and purchase history. These findings help provide insights into customer spending habits and factors that may contribute to higher customer value.

1A. Distribution of Total Spend by Membership Type

View selected R code
customer <- customer %>%
  mutate(MembershipType = factor(MembershipType,
                                 levels = c("Bronze", "Silver", "Gold")))

ggplot(customer, aes(x = MembershipType, y = TotalSpend, fill = MembershipType)) +
  geom_violin(trim = FALSE) +
  geom_boxplot(width = 0.1, fill = "white", alpha = 0.7) +
  labs(title = "Distribution of Total Spend by Membership Type",
       x = "Membership Type", y = "Total Spend ($)") +
  theme(legend.position = "none")
Graph Interpretation: This violin plot shows how total spending varies across Bronze, Silver, and Gold membership levels. Gold members consistently spend the most, followed by Silver and Bronze members. The clear separation between the groups suggests that customers with higher membership levels tend to make larger purchases and contribute more revenue.

5. Impact of Discount Application on Total Spend

Graph Interpretation: This violin plot shows the distribution of total spending for customers who received discounts and those who did not. Customers without discounts had a higher average spend ($909.01) compared to customers who received discounts ($787.27). The wider spread of higher spending values among non-discounted purchases indicates that larger transactions were more common in this group. These findings suggest that receiving a discount was not associated with increased spending, as customers paying full price tended to spend more overall.

6. Age Distribution by Membership Type

Graph Interpretation: The histogram shows how customer ages are distributed across membership levels. Bronze members are generally older, with most customers falling between ages 36 and 43. Silver members are concentrated between ages 31 and 35, while Gold members are primarily between ages 26 and 30. These results suggest that younger customers are more likely to hold higher membership levels, while older customers are more commonly associated with Bronze memberships.

7. Total Spend by Satisfaction Level

Graph Interpretation: This boxplot compares spending patterns across satisfaction groups. Customers who reported being satisfied spent substantially more than neutral or unsatisfied customers. The results suggest a positive relationship between customer satisfaction and purchasing behavior.

8. Total Spend by Gender

Graph Interpretation: This chart compares spending patterns between male and female customers. Male customers have higher average spending ($986.93) and median spending ($800.90) than female customers, whose average and median spending are $707.04 and $505.75, respectively. The boxplot also shows that spending values for male customers are generally shifted higher than those for female customers. These results indicate that male customers contribute more total spending on average within this dataset.

9. Items Purchased by City

Graph Interpretation: The boxplot shows clear differences in purchasing behavior across cities. Customers in San Francisco purchased the highest number of items, followed by New York. Los Angeles and Miami displayed moderate purchasing activity, while Chicago and Houston had the lowest numbers of items purchased. The variation within each city also differs, with some cities showing a wider spread of purchasing behavior than others. Overall, the results suggest that customer purchasing activity is strongest in San Francisco and New York and lower in Chicago and Houston.

Conclusion

This analysis identified several factors associated with customer spending behavior. Membership type, customer satisfaction, purchase frequency, and the number of items purchased all showed strong relationships with total spending. Gold members, satisfied customers, and customers who purchased more items generally spent the most, while spending tended to decrease as the time since the last purchase increased.

Customer demographics such as age, gender, and location were also associated with differences in purchasing behavior. Younger customers were more likely to hold higher membership levels, while spending patterns varied across cities and between gender groups. Together, these findings demonstrate how customer characteristics and purchasing habits can be used to better understand customer value and support data-driven business decisions.
